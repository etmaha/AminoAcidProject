About Essential Amino Acids

Protein is the building block of every cell in your body. Your body breaks down protein into amino acids and reorganizes them based on what your body needs might be. Your body uses twenty amino acids, and out of the twenty, there are nine amino acids that are essential, meaning you must get them from the diet. The other eleven amino acids can by synthesized from the nine essential amino acids.

All nine essential amino acids can be found in animal protein, but when it comes to plant proteins, normally they must be combined to make them a complete protein. This can pose a big problem to those following a vegan or vegetarian diet. There are a few plant proteins that are complete proteins, such as chia seeds, hemp, buckwheat, spirulina, amaranth, and quinoa. But the rest must be combined to get the full benefit.

Combinations of plant proteins that make them a whole protein include rice and beans, grains and legumes, or even peanut butter and wheat bread. The trick is to find the limiting amino acid in one plant protein, and find the other plant protein that is high in this amino acid. For instance, beans are high in lysine but low in methionine, but rice is low in lysine but high in methionine. This makes rice and beans a complete protein when eaten together, as they now have all nine essential amino acids for the human body.

Essential amino acids include phenylalanine, valine, threonine, tryptophan, isoleucine, methionine, histidine, leucine, and lysine. Non-essential amino acids include alanine, aspartic acid, asparagine, glutamic acid, and serine. Conditionally essential amino acids, meaning they are needed during times when great cell regeneration is needed, such as adolescence, trauma, recovery, or pregnancy, include arginine, cysteine, glutamine, glycine, proline, and tyrosine.
